Step one

Find out if an estate has already been opened

Connecticut estates are handled by the probate court for the town where the person lived — not necessarily where they died. Bloomfield falls under the Tobacco Valley Probate Court (District 3), based in Windsor Locks.

  1. Call the court directly and ask whether a file has been opened for Stanley's estate. This is public information — anyone can ask.
  2. If a file exists, ask who was appointed executor or administrator, and whether a will was submitted. Estate records, including a filed will, can be requested for a small copy fee.
  3. If no file exists yet, ask what's required to open one — usually a certified death certificate and an application, which any next of kin (including a sibling) can typically initiate if no one else has.

If there's no will: what happens next

This is worth understanding clearly, even though it may not be the answer the family wants to hear. Connecticut's intestacy law (the rules that apply when someone dies without a valid will) follows a strict order of priority:

  1. Surviving spouse and children first. Stanley was not married, but he has a legal daughter, Destiny — and adopted children inherit exactly as biological children do under Connecticut law.
  2. Parents, only if there is no surviving spouse or child.
  3. Siblings, only if there is no surviving spouse, child, or parent.
What this likely means here Because Stanley has a surviving legal child, Connecticut intestacy law would generally direct his estate to Destiny if no will exists — not to his siblings. This isn't a final answer (a will, if one turns up, could say something different), but it's the realistic starting expectation.

That said, being a sibling doesn't mean Gloria has no standing at all. Next of kin are generally entitled to notice that an estate has been opened, and can raise questions with the court about how it's being handled — even without a right to inherit.
